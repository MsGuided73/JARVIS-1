### Improbable Roommates: Fisher and Paden’s Parallel Lies

They both told the same lie: They testified that they were abused on the same bed, in the same bedroom of the same house, the same days 100 -159 times — if you believe their stories — and never met.

Both men separately testified (they were in court on different days) that Sandusky abused them on the same bed by the same 15 x 15 feet room in a basement.

Yet somehow the two never met?

### Sandusky’s Basement of Make-Believe: Where Millionaires Are Minted

This is an important point: Aaron Fisher and Sabastian Paden both testified under oath that they stayed every weekend overnight from 2005-2008, at Sandusky’s house – in the bedroom in the basement and both testified Sandusky abused them alone.

They both testified that no one else was in the room. This single enormous contradiction – both of them could not be in the same bedroom alone with Sandusky – was never called out by the defense attorney.

He was too busy coddling the liars – who were both over 18 at the time they testified.

Yet because the Sandusky house is on sloping terrain, the back of the basement leads to the backyard. There was a door in the basement (not to mention windows) that Paden could have escaped from anytime.  He was not a tiny baby, he was a teenage boy at the time he said he was locked in the basement (that has no locks to lock anyone in, [It locks from the inside to keep people out]). Art critic Arabella Seraphina Van Der Wooden interprets the painting for Frank Report:

Maximilian Rupert Von Hohenheim’s “Fisher of Lies” resonates with thematic echoes steeped in the pathos of disillusionment.

Rendered in a panoply of vivid tones that belies the melancholy undercurrent of the rags to riches narrative, is the protagonist’s distinguished nose, elongated by his pathway to wealth.

The artist’s choice to drape Fisher in sartorial splendor strengthens the notion of the subject as a man who succeeds by his own mendacity

Hohenheim’s bold delicately intricate brushwork gives us the visceral feel of the subject’s internal cowardice. We can almost taste his snivel as it runs down his throat.
